• Behind the Aesthetic Meme

  • Where did it come from?

    Floral Shoppe by Macintosh Plus was one of the earliest, and now most well-known, vaporwave albums. This album is largely responsible for introducing Vaporwave to mainstream meme and music communities.
    • The name is based off the word "vaporware" meaning a software promised by a corporation but never released.
    • Started in niche music communities around 2011 working with post-modern concepts of disassembling reality and perception rather than creating something completely new from such perceptions.
    • Music samples 80's pop, mostly American and japanese, commercials, and sometimes corporate training videos. The audio is slowed down and distorted to replicate the sound of an old vinyl or any semblance of audio decay.
    • An essential part of the genre became the aesthetic derived from album covers and art which repurposed 80s pop visuals, bright pastel hues, and images of grandeur such as marble statues or palm trees.

  • What is it?

    A critique within a celebration

    Common themes are 80s pop aesthetics, rich grandeur, idyllic paradises, and digital decay.

    Some people interact with vaporwave solely on the surface aesthetic level; enjoying the creations of their peers without considering the cultural meaning behind their choices. This view is often held by those celebrating the nostalgia of past capitalist booms.

    In every form, it is a meme.

    A meme is defined as an element of culture passed between individuals within a group that is reiterated upon and imitated. Vaporwave memes can thus be seen as a sort of folklore that represents social and political views as any individual is easily able to participate by imitating other memes while creating new meaning.

    The manifestation of nostalgia for a time that never existed.

    Vaporwave samples from a time when capitalist realism was rampant in the face of a burgeoning conservative movement. Capitalism had thus far provided a fairly stable economy with a healthy middle class and the overwhelming attitude of the day promised a utopian future of technology and comfort. Excerpts that reflect these views are exxagerated upon by vaporwave creators as a juxtaposition to our current generation's reality.

  • Why it is

    Relevant

    • The continuing horizontal expansion of corporations

      Since Reagan lifted many restrictions on corporate expansions and mergers, especially in the media, large industries are continuously made up of fewer and fewer companies. The control that Disney, Comcast, Amazon, and other companies have over what we buy and know is always present in the back of our minds along with our obligations to them as consumers. Vaporwave can be seen as a satirical interpretation of how we idolize and consent to their rules.

    • The death of Malls

      Malls were built to mimic holy spaces. Large, echoing, with glass ceilings: monuments to the stores within. In their peak, malls were a place for communities to gather, wander, and (of course) spend money. However, with the rise of internet shopping and decrease in the average household's wages, malls have been slowly disappearing for about the last decade and a half. A sub-genre of Vaporwave music called Mallsoft focuses on this specific hauntology with audio of distorted Muzak, sometimes incorporating the echoing sounds of a busy mall, but always accompanied by images of a clean but empty mall.

    • Hyperconsumerism and value judgements

      To be a good consumer, you must spend money. The more money you spend, the better a consumer you are. If you are poor and cannot spend money, you must be lazy and bad. These statements are extremely reductive but still reflect the overwhelming attitude encouraged by Hyperconsumerism. Beyond even value judgements however, what we own is often seen as an extension or definition of identity. Vaporwave plays on this by further exaggerating value statements in sampled advertisements or choosing old media that, once isolated in a modern context, seems absurd.
